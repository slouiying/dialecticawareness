Dialect Awareness for Educators

1. Why Dialect Awareness Matters

The term ‘dialect’ has long carried a negative connotation of language use that is lesser than that of an accepted standard (Hollmann, 2021). It is important to know that all languages are in fact groups of varieties, and each variety has its own rich history, cultural identity, as well as characteristic pronunciation, vocabulary, and grammatical structures (What Makes Language…A Language – Martin Hilpert, n.d.). All dialects are rule-governed and valid linguistic systems (Sociolinguistics: Crash Course Linguistics #7, n.d.).

Canada has eight distinct language regions in English-speaking Canada, including Aboriginal English, Cape Breton English, Lunenburg English, Newfoundland English, Ottawa Valley English, Pacific West Coast English, Quebec English, and Inland Canadian English (Marsh, 2018).

“Recognizing and valuing dialectical diversity in the classroom is an issue of equity and linguistic justice.” (Blundon, 2016)

 

2. Factors That Influence Linguistic Variety

 

Many different social and personal factors influence the way people speak. Understanding these can help educators respond more effectively and empathetically in linguistically diverse classrooms.

 

Standard Canadian English – the English used in most schools across Canada – can be somewhat or vastly different to the language used at home by many Canadian students. It holds a privileged place in society, not because it is more complete, or has better rules, or produces better thinkers, but because of politics and power (Walsh, 2018). Standard Canadian English may not align well the cultural values or language use of some students (Blundon, 2016).

Scenario: A student fails to make eye contact during a conversation with the classroom teacher. Rather than assuming disinterest or disrespect, the teacher recognizes that eye contact norms are a pragmatic dialectical feature. (Blundon, 2016)

4. What Might Teachers Observe in Students’ Nonstandard Language Use?

Teachers may find that students who use nonstandard dialects may have difficulty with school readiness concepts, as well as vocabulary for time, direction, position, quantity, and sequence (Blundon, 2016). Reading aloud may be challenging for students due to mis-mapping of sounds to letters (Blundon, 2016). Students may use different sound patterns, vocabulary, or grammatical constructions. Teachers might hear uninflected verbs, unique word orders, or different uses of articles and pronouns (Blundon, 2016). Furthermore, the differences between sound systems and the pronunciation of words may cause spelling problems for students (Blundon, 2016).

Scenario: A student says “he was badly scratch and biting.” Rather than correcting harshly, the teacher notes the grammar variation and gently restates: “Yes, he was badly scratched and bitten.” (Blundon, 2016)

 

5. Supporting Students Through Responsive Practices

Culturally responsive pedagogy, language affirmation, and strategic literacy support are all research-backed approaches for success in helping students bridge nonstandard English variations with the standard English they need to master in classrooms. Teachers should validate home languages (Blundon, 2016), promote positive language identity (Sociolinguistics: Crash Course Linguistics #7, n.d.), and explicitly teach language variation as a strength (Slang and Standard English with Rob Drummond, 2019).

Scenario: During a writing lesson, the teacher invites discussion about different ways of saying the same idea. One student says “She be happy.” The teacher uses this moment to teach about habitual aspect in some dialects. (Blundon, 2016)

 

6. Steps Teachers Can Take to Empower Student Language and Literacy Development

  • Learn about the dialects spoken in your classroom.
  • Use literature that features diverse voices and dialects.
  • Engage students in discussions about language variation and identity.
  • Give students time to acquire and apply standard English.
  • Model and explicitly teach code-switching.
  • Design assessments that are dialect neutral.
  • Collaborate with families and communities to learn language norms.
  • Reflect on your own language attitudes and implicit biases.

(Blundon, 2016)

Scenario: When planning a reading unit, a teacher selects a novel that includes Indigenous English dialects and prepares pre-reading activities that introduce students to those varieties. (Blundon, 2016)
